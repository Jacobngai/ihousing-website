## Why Guest Screening Matters in Malaysia

Malaysia's Airbnb market is different from Western countries. We have unique challenges:

- **Party culture:** Many young Malaysians and Singaporeans book Airbnbs specifically for gatherings that would violate hotel rules.
- **Local short stays:** Unlike tourists who book for weeks, many bookings are 1-2 nights â€” higher turnover means more risk.
- **Strata regulations:** Your building's JMB can fine you if guests cause problems. Some condos have banned Airbnb entirely due to owner complaints.
- **No security deposits enforced:** Airbnb removed the security deposit feature in 2022. You now rely on AirCover (insurance), which is notoriously difficult to claim.

Bottom line: Your first line of defense is *who you accept*.

## The iHousing Guest Screening Process (7 Steps)

Here's our exact vetting process. It takes 2-3 minutes per inquiry but saves us thousands in damages.

### 1. Check Profile Completeness

**Red flags:**
- No profile photo (or generic stock image)
- Account created in the last week
- Zero reviews (not a dealbreaker alone, but combined with other red flags...)
- No verified ID or government-issued document

**Green flags:**
- Profile photo showing a real person
- Account age > 1 year
- Verified phone + email + government ID
- Bio filled out (even just 1-2 sentences)

**What we do:** New accounts with no reviews aren't automatically rejected, but they get extra scrutiny in the next steps.

---

### 2. Read Their Reviews (As a Guest)

This is your goldmine. Read what *other hosts* said about them.

**Red flags:**
- Multiple hosts mention "left the place dirty"
- Any comment about "unauthorized guests" or "party"
- "Damaged property" or "violated house rules"
- Disputes over checkout times or early arrival requests

**Green flags:**
- "Clean," "respectful," "followed all rules"
- Multiple reviews from superhosts
- Hosts would "welcome them back anytime"

**What we do:** If we see even *one* negative review mentioning parties or damage, we decline. No exceptions.

---

### 3. Analyze the Booking Details

Certain booking patterns are statistically higher risk:

**Red flags:**
- **Same-day or next-day booking:** Impulse bookings (often parties).
- **Local booking:** Malaysian or Singaporean booking a Melaka property for 1 night? Very high party risk.
- **Friday/Saturday night, 1-2 nights:** Prime party window.
- **Mismatched guest count:** Books for 2 but asks "Can 6 of us fit?"
- **Just turned 18-22 years old (visible on profile):** Young + local + weekend = party red flag.

**Green flags:**
- Booking 2+ weeks in advance
- International traveler staying 3+ nights
- Family booking (couple with kids)
- Business traveler (weekday stays, professional profile)

**What we do:** We don't reject local bookings outright, but we require additional verification (see Step 4).

---

### 4. Read Their Message Carefully

How they communicate tells you everything.

**Red flags:**
- **"Is there parking for 5 cars?"** (You accommodate 2 guests... why 5 cars?)
- **"Are the neighbors close?"** or **"Can we be loud?"** (Party.)
- **"Do you have a doorman/security?"** (They know they're breaking rules.)
- **Excessive questions about cancellation policy:** Planning to back out?
- **Poor grammar/spelling in English + Malaysian:** Could indicate they didn't read your listing.

**Green flags:**
- Asks about check-in logistics, WiFi, nearby food â€” normal tourist questions.
- Mentions they read your house rules and agree to them.
- Provides context: "We're visiting for a wedding at XYZ Hotel."

**What we do:** If their message raises flags, we send a pre-screening message (see Step 5).

---

### 5. Send a Pre-Screening Message

For medium-risk bookings (local + weekend + few reviews), we send this:

> "Hi [Name], thanks for your interest! Just a few quick questions before I confirm:  
> 1. What brings you to Melaka?  
> 2. Will anyone else be joining you during your stay?  
> 3. Have you read our house rules (no parties, no smoking, quiet hours after 10pm)?  
> 
> Looking forward to hosting you!"

**Red flag responses:**
- No answer (they ignore the message)
- Vague answer: "Just visiting."
- Defensive: "Why are you asking so many questions?"
- Contradictory: "Just 2 of us" (but they asked about parking for 5 cars earlier)

**Green flag responses:**
- Specific: "We're here for a friend's wedding at Hatten Hotel."
- Reassuring: "Yes, read the rules â€” we're a quiet couple, just want to explore Jonker Walk."

**What we do:** If they don't reply within 12 hours, we decline. If the answer is vague, we follow up with one more question. Still vague? Decline.

## How to Politely Decline a Booking

You can't say "You look like a partier" (discrimination). Instead:

**Template 1 (Vague but effective):**
> "Hi [Name], unfortunately we have a scheduling conflict with your dates. I've had to decline your request. Wishing you all the best finding another place!"

**Template 2 (If they violated a rule):**
> "Hi [Name], I noticed your inquiry mentions bringing 8 guests, but our max capacity is 4 per fire safety regulations. I can't accommodate your group size. Thanks for understanding!"

**Template 3 (For pushy guests):**
> "Hi [Name], based on your questions, I don't think our property is the right fit for your needs. I'm going to decline so you can find a better match. All the best!"

**Pro tip:** Decline within 24 hours to avoid the "non-responsive host" flag.

## When Good Guests Go Bad (Damage Control)

Even with perfect screening, problems happen. Here's our protocol:

### If You Suspect a Party in Progress:
1. **Message immediately:** "Hi, we've received a noise complaint. Please keep it down or I'll have to ask you to leave."
2. **Document everything:** Screenshots, photos of crowded parking (if visible), timestamps.
3. **Call Airbnb Safety Line:** 24/7 hotline for hosts. They can authorize early checkout.

### After a Bad Experience:
1. **File AirCover claim within 14 days** (damage protection).
2. **Leave an honest review** â€” warn other hosts.
3. **Update your house rules** if you spotted a gap.
